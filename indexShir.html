Maharashtra

About Maharashtra

Maharashtra is a state in western India, known for its rich cultural heritage, powerful history, thriving economy, and diverse geography. It is the second-most populous and third-largest state in India by area and Shirdi, a spiritual town in western Maharashtra, lies in the Ahmednagar district, approximately 83 km northeast of Ahmednagar and around 250 km from Mumbai. It is a globally renowned pilgrimage destination, primarily known as the final abode of Sai Baba of Shirdi, a saint revered across religions for his life of simplicity, miracles, and teachings on faith and compassion.

Though once a sleepy village, Shirdi transformed into a major spiritual and tourism hub after the 20th century, when Sai Baba’s fame spread beyond Maharashtra to all of India and abroad. Today, Shirdi receives over 25 million pilgrims annually, making it one of the most visited spiritual sites in India. Shirdi is often called the “Land of Sai”, where peace and devotion never sleep.

Maharashtra is also a land of agro-cultural fusion, where the deep-rooted traditions of Warkari pilgrimages coexist with some of the most innovative agricultural practices in India. The state's Vidarbha region is known not only for cotton farming but also for its distinct food traditions, including spicy saoji cuisine. In the rural heartlands, folk storytelling forms like ‘Powada’ and Tamasha theatre continue to thrive, often blending music, social themes, and satire. Maharashtra is also one of the few states that balances sugarcane cooperatives, wine tourism in Nashik, and agri-tech startups all under one roof—showing how tradition and technology walk hand in hand. The state’s unique fort tourism circuit, involving treks across Shivaji-era hill forts like Rajgad, Torna, and Raigad, attracts both history lovers and adventure seekers, offering immersive experiences not just in heritage but also in resilience.

History of Maharashtra

The historical significance of Shirdi stems not from ancient dynasties or architecture, but from the spiritual presence of Sai Baba, who lived in the village for over 60 years (c. 1858–1918). His teachings of religious unity, love for all, and “Sabka Malik Ek” (One God governs all) attracted both Hindus and Muslims during his time — and continue to do so today. Baba lived a life of simplicity, residing in Dwarkamai, a dilapidated mosque that he turned into a sacred space where all were welcome regardless of caste or creed.

The Samadhi Mandir, where Baba’s mortal remains were laid to rest, was built by a wealthy devotee and now forms the core of the temple complex. Nearby, Dwarkamai, Chavadi, Gurusthan, and Lendi Baug are all preserved with deep reverence and maintained with the utmost care by the Shri Sai Baba Sansthan Trust.
