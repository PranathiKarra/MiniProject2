Kedarnath Temple

About Kedarnath Temple

Kedarnath Temple is one of the most revered Hindu shrines dedicated to Lord Shiva, located in the majestic Himalayan region of Uttarakhand, India. Positioned near the Mandakini River at an altitude of 3,583 meters, it is part of the Char Dham Yatra and is believed to have been built by the Pandavas and later revived by Adi Shankaracharya in the 8th century.

The temple is surrounded by snow-capped peaks and picturesque landscapes, attracting lakhs of pilgrims and adventure seekers every year. It is accessible only for six months due to extreme weather. Kedarnath holds immense spiritual value, especially during Maha Shivaratri and the annual opening and closing ceremonies.

Architecture of the Temple

  1. Kedarnath Temple is built from massive stone slabs arranged in a pyramidal structure.
  2. The temple is made in the traditional North Indian Himalayan style of architecture without using any mortar.
  3. The temple’s Garbha Griha houses the sacred Shivalinga – a conical rock formation representing Lord Shiva.
  4. The temple was constructed in a way that it survived multiple natural calamities, including the 2013 floods.

Facts About Kedarnath Temple

  1. One of the 12 Jyotirlingas: Kedarnath is among the 12 holiest Shiva shrines, making it a crucial pilgrimage site.
  2. Mythical Connection: It is believed that the Pandavas built the temple to seek forgiveness from Shiva after the Kurukshetra war.
  3. Burial Site of Adi Shankaracharya: A samadhi of the great saint is located behind the temple.
  4. Closed During Winters: The idol of Lord Shiva is shifted to Ukhimath during the harsh winter months.
  5. Survived 2013 Floods: Despite devastating floods, the temple remained miraculously intact, protected by a large boulder behind it.

About Lord Kedarnath (Shiva)

Lord Shiva, known as Kedarnath in this form, is considered the protector and destroyer in the Hindu Trinity. The name “Kedarnath” is derived from "Kedar," meaning field, and "Nath," meaning Lord – thus, “Lord of the Field.”

In mythology, after the war of Mahabharata, the Pandavas sought Lord Shiva to absolve their sins. Shiva, unwilling to forgive them easily, disguised himself as a bull. Bhima recognized the bull and tried to catch it, but the bull vanished into the earth. Later, Shiva appeared in his divine form and forgave them.

It is believed that different parts of Shiva's body appeared in different locations: the hump at Kedarnath, arms at Tungnath, face at Rudranath, navel at Madhyamaheshwar, and hair at Kalpeshwar – collectively known as Panch Kedar.
