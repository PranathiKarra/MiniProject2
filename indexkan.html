About Kanchi

Kanchipuram, city, northeastern Tamil Nadu state, southern India. It is located on the banks of the Vegavathi River, about 45 miles (72 km) southwest of Chennai. Kanchipuram is one of the seven sacred Moksha-puris in Hinduism and is considered one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in India. Renowned as the City of a Thousand Temples, it is especially famous for the Kamakshi Amman Temple, a major center of Shakti worship and spiritual heritage in South India.

Kanchipuram is popularly known as “The City of Thousand Temples” (Aayiram Kovilgalin Nagaram in Tamil), reflecting its deep-rooted spiritual and architectural heritage. This sacred city resonates with timeless devotion and is one of the seven Moksha-puris—holy cities believed to grant liberation. While it offers a peaceful, temple-centric atmosphere, Kanchipuram also provides the essential comforts of a traditional yet thriving town. Its origins date back to at least the 2nd century BCE, with references found in early Tamil Sangam literature, making it one of the oldest cultural and religious centers in South India.

Known as the “City of Learning”, Kanchipuram was a prominent center of spiritual and scholarly activity in ancient South India. Once the capital of the Pallava dynasty, it is renowned for temples like the Kamakshi Amman Temple and its contributions to art, philosophy, and religious studies. The city finds mention in ancient Tamil texts and attracted saints, scholars, and philosophers from across the country, making it a key hub of Vedantic and cultural learning for centuries.

The ancient history of Kanchipuram is closely linked to the Pallava dynasty, who made it their capital between the 4th and 9th centuries CE. Later, it came under the rule of the Cholas, Vijayanagara kings, Muslim sultanates, and eventually the British. Despite changing rulers, Kanchipuram remained a vital spiritual and cultural center, with the Kamakshi Amman Temple continuing as a prominent seat of Shakti worship and religious significance throughout the centuries.

Kanchipuram holds a rich cultural and spiritual legacy dating back over 2,000 years. It was the capital of the Pallava dynasty, followed by the Cholas, and later came under the rule of the Vijayanagara Empire and other regional powers. Despite these transitions, the city remained a revered center of learning and devotion. The Kamakshi Amman Temple, a major shrine of Shakti worship, flourished through these eras and continues to be the spiritual heart of Kanchipuram.

History of Kanchi

According to legend, Kanchipuram was once a forested area known as Brahma Kshetra, where Lord Vishnu is believed to have performed penance. It is said that Goddess Parvati, in the form of Kamakshi, created a Shiva Lingam out of sand and worshipped it under a mango tree near a sacred tank, seeking union with Lord Shiva. Pleased by her devotion, Shiva appeared and agreed to marry her, leading to their divine union in Kanchipuram. Over time, the forested area was transformed into a spiritual city, with the Kamakshi Amman Temple at its heart, making it one of the most sacred Shakti centers in India.

Bangaru Kamakshi was the name of an old picture of Kamakshi in gold, standing and praying. When it was anticipated that the temple would be attacked, it was moved to Thanjavur.
